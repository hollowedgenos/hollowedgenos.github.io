Released over 10 years ago on Steam, Splice is a quaint, but engagingly enthralling puzzle game. The player is put into a world of bacteria. Each level is a puzzle consisting of an arrangement of these capsule-shaped microbes. The player must use their brain to move these microbes into a certain formation to complete a puzzle.

---

# Unasked for Exposition...
{: .text-center}

So, before I talk about Splice, I have an interesting story to tell pertaining to how I acquired this game. 

It was ten or maybe eleven years ago, at the time I played a lot of Team Fortress 2. When I say I played a lot of it, I mean A LOT. I mostly frequented trading servers. For those who don’t know, Team Fortress 2 has an actual economy based around cosmetic items (i.e., hats) that one can customize their characters with. Well, trade servers are where people go to buy and sell items. It’s a really long-winded way of explaining this but here’s the deal. I was in a trade server and there was a guy on the voice chat who said that if anyone could answer a question correctly before anyone else, they’d get a free game. Long story short, I answered the question and got it right. This man gave me Splice. I remember playing it for about an hour before getting stuck on a puzzle and never touching it again. 

Until now.

# The REAL First Strand-Like Game
{: .text-center}

![A strand sequence puzzle in Splice.]({{ site.url }}{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/splice-gameplay_1-1.jpg){: .align-center}
*The player usually starts out with a sequence like this, which they must solve.*
{: .text-center .small}

Splice as a concept is fairly simple.

Each level is presented as a strand of microbes that must be moved to match the configuration outlined to the player. 

This sounds simple and it is, but there are elements that keep the game from being too easy. Each microbe is connected as if it were a tree list (I.e., the parent microbe has two microbes below it and so on), the player can move one or sections of these microbe trees to solve the puzzle. 

However, the number of times the player can move these microbes is limited, this number of moves varies for each puzzle. A puzzle can be reset if the player goes over the set number of moves. The player also has the option to use their mouse wheel as a fast-forward and rewind function. Mistakes can be rewound, and a different course of action can be made. It really adds to the casual experience which I’m all for.

![A more complex strand sequence puzzle.]({{ site.url }}{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/splice-gameplay_1-2.jpg){: .align-center}
*Players will be challenged with more complex sequences as they progress.*
{: .text-center .small}

The atmosphere of Splice is relaxing and serene. The player is transported to a soft-pastel void full of microbes. The music adds to the vibe of the game as well, taking the form of a soft sounding piano that plays in the distance.  

Overall, Splice is a great game for those that like to kick back, relax, and challenge their brain with ever increasingly difficult puzzles. I definitely recommend it to puzzle game enthusiasts!
