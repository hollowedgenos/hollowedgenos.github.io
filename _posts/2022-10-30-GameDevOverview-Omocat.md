# Preface
{: .text-center}

April 21st, 2014, a Kickstarter campaign launches for an indie role-playing game, Omori. In just a few hours, the campaign reaches half of its $22,000 goal. The game reached full funding and made its first stretch goal of $30,000 three days later on April 24th. 

But that’s not the end of the story, on May 5th the game reached its second stretch goal of $50,000 (OMOCAT, 2014a; 2014b; 2014c). The demolishing of stretch goals became a trend for the entirety of Omori’s campaign on Kickstarter. As on May 13th, the game reached $65,000 dollars (OMOCAT, 2014d). The numbers only kept pumping up exponentially from there. By June 5th, the last day of the campaign, 5,910 backers funded Omori with a whopping total of $203,300 (OMOCAT, 2014e).

![Screenshot from Omori Kickstarter Page]({{ site.url }}{{ site.baseurl }}/assets/images/omori-kickstarter-page.jpg){: .align-center}
*Kickstarter page for Omori.*
{: .text-center .small}

Originally, Omori was slated to be released May 2015. It would take six years of development for the game to release on Steam, December 25th, 2020 (OMOCAT, 2014a; OMOCAT LLC, 2020).

Now I could ramble on and on about the extenuating circumstances that contributed to Omori’s long development cycle. Heck, I could babble about how Omori is one of my favorite games of all time, how rich it is from a gameplay and narrative perspective. The juxtaposition between cutesy art style versus its dark themes and presentation of psychological horror. How Omori emotionally wrecks you, refusing to leave your mind long after finishing it. However, that is not what I am here to talk about. What I do want to talk about is the game’s creator, who goes by the pseudonym Omocat.

# A Game Developer's Journey
{: .text-center}

Omocat is an artist that originally got their start posting illustrations and webcomics on their Tumblr blog during the early 2010s. They were assumably an art student during this time as well. While their educational background is not explicitly stated, the earliest posts on their Tumblr blog are illustrations labeled as being class projects (@_OMOCAT, 2010). In 2011, Omocat started a separate Tumblr blog called ‘omoriboy’. In which Omocat would upload illustrations of a monochrome boy, Omoriboy, who lived in a white void, called White Space, with his cat. Eventually, Omocat planned to make a full graphic novel of Omoriboy, but they thought the story was too linear. They wanted people to have an active role in exploring the narrative. Omocat came to a realization, “[They] needed to make OMORI a game.” (OMOCAT, 2014f). 

In 2014, Omocat started their own company, OMOCAT LLC (Bio — omocat, n.d.). This is also the same year the Omori Kickstarter campaign began, which as mentioned before, was a huge success. During Omori’s development, Omocat took many roles. This included illustrator, story writer, programmer, game designer and game director (About omocat, n.d.; OMOCAT video game credits and Biography, n.d.). Not to mention OMOCAT LLC. is a company that sells art and clothing. Which they were managing all while developing Omori. Being in a small indie company, that is a lot to take under your belt. Which is why I have so much respect and admiration for them.

# An Inspiration to DREAMERS Like Me
{: .text-center}

As an aspiring game designer who is uncertain about having my own ideas and dreams realized. Omocat is a shining light in that deep, dark abyss of uncertainty and doubt. The passion and heart put into Omori by Omocat and their talented team of people is clear and it moved me. I’ve always wanted to make video games, but playing Omori made me realize something. I want to create games that are not only fun but have a message. I want to create games that emotionally engage and resonate with people. Through Omori, Omocat taught me that to be a game developer is to put your very soul into the games you make. Through that, you touch the souls of others.
